1. Stat points gained per level (Classic 1–99)
In Classic RO (pre‑Renewal, 1–99):

Stat points gained going from BaseLv x to x+1:

StatPointsGained = floor(x / 5) + 3

Example: from 94 → 95: floor(94 / 5) + 3 = 18 + 3 = 21 points.

Total stat points from 1 → 99:

1225 points from leveling.

Plus extra starting points from character creation:

48 points invested during creation (the starting STR/AGI/etc).

Total usable stat budget (1–99 normal char): 1273 points.
​

Design takeaway for your game:

You can copy the same pattern: “few points early, more points per level as you go up”.

The formula floor(x/5)+3 is simple and scales smoothly.

2. Cost to raise a core stat (1 → 99)
Classic RO uses increasing cost per stat point:

Cost to raise a stat from value n to n+1:

Cost(n → n+1) = floor((n − 1) / 10) + 2.
​

Interpretation:

Stat 1 → 2 costs 2 points.

Stat 2 → 3 costs 2 points.

…

Stat 10 → 11 costs 3 points.

Stat 20 → 21 costs 4 points.

…

The cost increases by 1 every 10 stat points.
​

Total cost to reach 99 in one stat:

Roughly 120–130+ points worth of cost; in practice, with six stats, players spread points.
​

Design takeaway:

Yes, higher stats cost more: this is exactly how RO prevents 99 in everything.

The step function (every 10 points) is easy to implement and explain:

Stat 1–9: 2 points each

10–19: 3 points each

20–29: 4 points each

…

90–99: 11 points each

You can tune these base numbers (start at 2 and +1 every 10) to make your game more or less “grindy” in stat building.

3. How “leveling up core stats” feels to the player
In practice, in RO:

Early levels:

You get few stat points per level, but each stat point is cheap.

Players can quickly push their main stat to 20–40.
​

Mid levels:

Per‑level stat gain rises, but each stat step becomes more expensive.

Players feel they must “commit” to a build (AGI build, VIT tank, etc).
​

Late levels:

You gain a lot of stat points per level (20+), but each extra stat point can cost 8–11 points.

Most builds reach “one or two stats at 90+” rather than everything high.
​

Design takeaway:

This structure naturally creates identity builds (full AGI, full VIT, etc) without needing explicit class restrictions.

Extra stat points at high levels make level‑ups still feel meaningful even late game.

4. Base EXP curve (Classic 1–99)
Classic RO uses a steep, non‑linear EXP curve. Exact numbers are in the base EXP chart.
​

Key characteristics:

Levels 1–10:

Very low EXP requirements, quick early levels.

Levels 11–40:

Gradually rising EXP; still fairly fast.
​

Levels 41–80:

EXP per level starts to jump noticeably.

Levels 81–98:

Very steep; each level takes significantly more EXP than the last.

Level 98 → 99:

One of the biggest jumps; this is the “grind wall” that makes 99 special.
​

The curve is essentially exponential / super‑linear. If you want a simple model for your own game:

Example “RO‑inspired” formula for required EXP from level x to x+1:

RequiredEXP(x → x+1) ≈ A × x^B

A: base constant (sets overall pace)

B: exponent (e.g. 2.5–3.0 for strong late‑game grind)

Or copy the idea: handcrafted table with small numbers 1–10, medium 11–70, huge spike 80–99.
​

Design takeaway:

RO makes high level (99) feel like an achievement by making the last ~20 levels much slower.

You can mirror this structure but flatten it if you want a more casual game.

5. How to apply this to your game
If you want a “Classic‑like” feel:

Stat point gain per level:

Use: StatPointsGained = floor(Level / 5) + 3 (for 1–99).

This gives ≈1225 points total, enough for one or two 90+ stats plus secondary stats.

Stat cost:

Use: Cost(n → n+1) = floor((n − 1) / 10) + 2.

This ensures high stats are expensive and forces meaningful choices.
​

EXP curve:

Use a steep, late‑game‑heavy curve (handcrafted table or x^B function).

Make 1–30 fast, 30–80 moderate, 80–99 slow.
